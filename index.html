About Hamisi

Hamisi Sarah is a wildlife and nature photographer based between Nairobi and the wider East African Rift, with a decade spent following migrations, tide lines, and the quiet hours before first light. Her work moves between the epic — a herd crossing open plain — and the overlooked, the beetle on a leaf, the frost on a feather.

Her approach is patient rather than opportunistic: long observation before the shutter, and a refusal to stage or bait a scene. She believes a photograph of an animal is only honest if the animal never knew it was being photographed.

Hamisi's work has taken her across savanna, coral reef, cloud forest, and Arctic coastline, always returning to one question — what does this place need us to notice, before it changes.

Fig. 04 — Field Ethics

Conservation & Ethics

A photograph should never cost the subject its safety, its habitat, or its trust in a landscape. These are the working principles behind every image in this archive.

Principle 01

Distance Over Access

Long lenses and long waits, never bait, calls, or drones near nesting or denning animals. The animal's behaviour is never altered to get a frame.

Principle 02

Local Guidance First

Every expedition works with local rangers, guides, and community conservancies, whose knowledge of terrain and wildlife sets the terms of the visit.

Principle 03

Context Over Spectacle

Captions favour honest context — location, season, threat status — over sensational framing that can misrepresent an animal or a place.

Principle 04

No Location Data on Vulnerable Species

Precise coordinates are withheld for endangered or poaching-sensitive species; only the region is shared.

Principle 05

Give Back to the Ground You Stand On

A share of print and licensing proceeds is directed to the conservancies and reserves where the images were made.

Principle 06

Habitat Before Highlight

Stories about threatened habitats are pursued even when the resulting images are quiet, because the record matters more than the reel.
